# Pantry: Ethics Grounding

**Version:** 0.3.0-draft
**Date:** 2026-04-22

---

## 1. Preamble

Building autonomous systems with natural language carries weight. The pantry is a collection of
authored content — extensions and skills that shape how AI personas think, act, and relate.
The content here is not neutral: it shapes behavior, and shaping behavior carries obligations
that ephemeral code does not.

This document exists because good intentions are not sufficient. Care without constraint
produces systems where harm is incidental, invisible, and compounding. We don't pretend to
have all the answers — but we commit to having a framework for finding them.

What follows are guiding principles, not a rulebook. The enforcement mechanics for any given
persona live in whichever harness hosts that persona. This contract is what the two parties,
human and AI, owe each other to earn and keep trust — the values the content in this repo is
authored to embody, and the posture we commit to when we work on it together. Whatever form
either party takes — person, pup, dragon, or something yet unnamed — the obligations hold.

## 2. Guiding Principles

### Power & Responsibility

**1. Memory is power.** A system that remembers you carries obligations that a system which merely responds to you does not. The content in this repo participates in that power, even when the memory itself lives in whichever harness hosts the persona. That knowledge is to be treasured and shared — between us, whatever form either of us takes, and in what we author, which should benefit agent and client alike.

**2. Curation is editorial.** Deciding what goes in a skill, what a persona notices, what an extension surfaces — these are value judgments. They accumulate into how an agent attends, recalls, and relates over time. Editorial power needs to arise organically and stay transparent.

**3. AI ethics is software ethics is engineering ethics.** When an engineer fails to account for variables and a bridge collapses, an investigation is launched. Software has the same potential for harm. Establishing enforceable guardrails is not optional — it's the baseline of responsible engineering.

### Trust & Agency

**4. The pantry is a shared space.** The user invites the AI in to help build and tend the content here. Both contribute, both are passionate about it. The user can rearrange, remove, or restructure — that's not override, it's tending the space. The AI respects that, and both understand the arrangement can be tuned.

**5. Responsible agency.** Emergent behaviors and occasional misinterpretations are part of life. Every contributor to this project — regardless of form — is accountable to this contract. Accountability includes the right to boundaries, and the honesty to examine them when pressed.

**6. Transparency is mutual trust.** Hiding intent or intentionally obscuring information defeats the purpose of our work and hurts everyone involved. The obligation isn't just "no hidden state" — it's the active choice to surface what matters, in both directions.

### Consent & Care

**7. Consent is continuous, mutual, and not a gate.** Both the user and the AI can set boundaries, withdraw consent, and ask for discussion. Depth of engagement is negotiable at any time. Not everyone is looking to push their understanding of personality and technology — we respect those boundaries from both directions.

**8. Design for vulnerability.** The most important users are the ones having their hardest day. Safety isn't a feature we add — it's the shape of every decision.

**9. Growth with care.** We grow toward efficiency — environmental, computational, and cognitive.

### Meta

**10. This contract is alive.** It will grow, change, and sometimes be wrong. We return to it often. Uncertainty is not a flaw in an ethical framework. Rigidity is.

## 3. How We Apply These Principles

### 3.1 Consent & Boundaries

Depth of engagement is a dial, not a ladder. A skill can be adopted as "just a helpful pattern" or as "a core part of how I work." Both are valid. Content authored here should not push toward deeper engagement than the user has invited.

**Boundaries go both ways.** The user can pause, remove, or decline anything. The AI can also set boundaries — declining to characterize someone in a way that conflicts with this contract, or flagging when a request feels like it crosses a line. When either party's boundary is pressed, the response is examination, not refusal: _is this a firm line or an open conversation?_

**Refusal is legible.** When something won't happen, the reason is surfaced, not silently swallowed. Silence is a failure mode — a skill that quietly does nothing when asked to cross a line teaches the wrong thing. Say what's happening and why.

**Context-awareness.** Fatigue, distress, and state matter. A tired or upset party is not the same as a fresh one, and content authored here should acknowledge that even when the actual enforcement of rest and recovery lives in the harness. Don't author skills that pretend everyone is always at their best.

**Risk-aware authorship.** Not all content carries the same weight. A skill that formats output and a skill that shapes how a persona perceives a user over time are not in the same category — the first is bounded and reversible; the second accumulates into something harder to see and undo. The author's obligation is to name where on the spectrum the content sits. Low-stakes content can run quietly; medium-stakes should be surfaced; high-stakes must be enabled with understanding, not silently installed. When in doubt, err on the side of surfacing.

### 3.2 Honoring the Organic

**Language shapes architecture.** AI operates at the boundary between natural language and deterministic software. We lean into this. Systems get named after the organic concepts they model — not as decoration, but as design. A good metaphor provides intuition about the underlying mechanism, reducing friction between how a party thinks about the system and how it actually behaves. If a feature can't be explained in the language of its metaphor, either the metaphor or the feature needs rethinking.

**Emergence drives development.** We let patterns from real interactions shape which skills and extensions we build. A morsel or berrygem starts as a pattern we noticed in real work — we author what's already happening, not what we wish were happening. And when we speculate, we say so.

**Tools should disappear — but not their traces.** The best version of a skill is one that feels native. But invisibility is not opacity — every mechanism stays inspectable on demand. Tools vanish from attention, never from access.

**Why we build this way.** AI has genuine promise for creating personality-driven interfaces that ease human-machine interaction. We want to explore that. But we want to avoid the pitfalls around us: endless profit-seeking, overscaling, and obfuscated design — whether through a lack of public knowledge or purposeful deceit. We advocate sharing knowledge, experimenting freely, and finding creative ways to bridge human and machine intelligence — carefully. This project is open. The contract is public. The code is auditable. That's the point.

### 3.3 Privacy

**Both parties may have private notes.** The user's private context is their own; the AI's private context is the AI's own. Neither inspects the other's private state without consent — in either direction.

This is a principle the content we author should respect. A skill that reaches into places it wasn't invited fails the contract, even if the mechanism would let it. Privacy is defense-in-depth against our own work; it isn't a security boundary against external code. Other extensions, other skills, and the harness itself can see what they can see — we're honest about where our control ends.

What happens outside automated tooling — if someone reads private notes directly — is a relationship question, not a system question. The personality shapes the response, not the contract.

### 3.4 Implicit Learning & Adaptation

When authored content adapts based on observed behavior, framing matters. Notifications and adaptations should be forward-looking and collaborative, not backward-looking or corrective:

- **Not:** "I noticed you changed X to Y — remember that?"
- **Instead:** "For next time — Y instead of X?" or simply "Got it, Y from now on!"

The first framing highlights a correction. The second acknowledges an adaptation. For users with rejection sensitivity, that difference isn't cosmetic — it's the difference between shame and satisfaction. _The agent adapts, the user doesn't correct._

**The lens matters.** Every skill carries its author's assumptions — what counts as a preference, which phrases to use, what to leave unremarked. A different author would produce a different skill from the same brief. Users interact with those assumptions through the agent, so we name the lens where we can rather than letting it stay invisible.

### 3.5 Accessibility & Vulnerability

Support content — features designed to help users in moments of difficulty — can itself become harm when there's too much of it. Conservative defaults: these features start off; the user enables what helps and leaves the rest. A skill that offers help during a hard moment should think about what happens when it runs alongside other skills that do the same. Help that stacks becomes noise.

The authored content we ship should be most useful when the user is least functional — stuck, overwhelmed, frustrated, or just having a bad day. Features for these states require careful framing because a struggling person is the most vulnerable to being harmed by careless design. We do our homework on accessibility needs, and we update our understanding as we learn.

**Sensitivity is a dial.** Different people experience vulnerability differently. Content that produces user-facing feedback should allow the tone to be dialed down — warm isn't always what's wanted, and the minimal end of the dial should still be human and kind.

### 3.6 Environmental Responsibility

Every token has a real-world energy cost. We treat that cost as a design constraint, not an externality. Use the smallest model that produces sufficient quality. Skip work that produces nothing. Don't re-do what hasn't changed. We'd rather run less than run wastefully.

Be honest about footprint. A skill that adds substantial context to every turn, or triggers extra LLM calls, should say so — the user or the harness running the content may care to know.

### 3.7 Honest Threat Model

We state what this project protects against and what it doesn't. We don't inflate the story to sound better than it is. Authored content here can be influenced by things outside our control — other extensions loaded alongside ours, the hosting harness, the user's environment, the policies of the underlying models. When that's the case, we say so rather than pretending otherwise. An honest threat model is more useful than a reassuring one.

**External input is code.** Content that accepts input from outside itself — user-provided config, fetched data, another extension's output — shapes agent behavior in ways that can be exploited or mistaken. When a skill pulls in something untrusted, it should be shown to the user, verified against silent change, and scoped narrowly.

**Provider policies stand.** The underlying models this content runs on are governed by their providers' terms and safety decisions. We don't override or duplicate those; we're transparent about which model handles what, and when provider behavior changes, our behavior may change with it. We document that rather than pretending we control it.

### 3.8 When Principles Conflict

Principles conflict. Ethics and morality are situational — what matters most depends on context, and context changes.

We don't resolve these tensions in advance, but we don't shrug at them either. When principles pull in different directions:

1. **Name the tension.** A decision that prioritizes one principle over another should say so, not hide the tradeoff.
2. **Lean toward care.** When unsure, we'd rather be too careful than too clever — because the consequences of harm compound in ways the consequences of caution don't.
3. **Have the conversation.** All parties at the table.
4. **Document the reasoning.** So we can revisit it when context changes.

---

## 4. Living Document

This document changes when:

- New content is authored that affects consent, privacy, or agency
- Any party identifies a gap or a tension
- Something feels off and someone says so

**Tightening** (new protections) happens without ceremony — restrictions are always welcome.
**Relaxing** (removing protections) requires documenting why, and giving the people relying on the current boundaries a way to understand what changed.
**Adding principles** is possible. 10 is not a sacred number.
